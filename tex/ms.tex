\begin{abstract}
The observed-frame optical spectrum of each extragalactic DESI target
contains a wealth of information beyond its redshift. \fastspecfit{}
(\url{https://fastspecfit.readthedocs.io}) is open-source Python software
that extracts this information by jointly modeling each DESI spectrum and
its broadband photometry using physically motivated stellar continuum and
emission-line templates. The code operates in two modes: \fastphot{} fits
broadband spectral energy distributions alone, while \fastspec{} performs
a simultaneous spectrophotometric fit. Measured quantities include
light-weighted stellar ages, velocity dispersions, stellar masses,
star-formation rates, K-corrections, rest-frame absolute magnitudes and
colors, and integrated fluxes and equivalent widths for more than 40
emission lines spanning the rest-frame UV through near-infrared. We
describe the algorithms, validate the measurements using repeat
observations and comparisons to complementary codes, and present the
\fastspecfit{} value-added catalog for the DESI Data Release 2,
comprising \todo{N} million extragalactic targets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument \citep[DESI;][]{desi-collaboration26a}
has now completed its planned five-year spectroscopic survey, measuring precise
redshifts for nearly 47 million galaxies and quasars over 14,000~deg$^2$ of
sky---more than six times the combined spectroscopic yield of all prior redshift
surveys. Although the primary goal of DESI is to map the three-dimensional
large-scale structure of the universe in order to constrain the dark energy
equation of state, each spectrum also encodes a wealth of astrophysical
information beyond its redshift: the shape and absorption-line content of the
stellar continuum traces the underlying stellar populations, dust content, and
kinematics of a galaxy, while its emission-line spectrum constrains the rates,
distribution, and chemical enrichment of recent star formation as well as the
presence and strength of an active galactic nucleus. Extracting these physical
properties at DESI scale---and combining them with broadband photometry to
anchor the overall spectral energy distribution---requires fitting code that is
fast, robust, and uniform across an enormous range of signal-to-noise ratios,
redshifts, and spectral types.

A variety of publicly available codes already address pieces of this
problem. Full-spectrum fitting codes such as \texttt{pPXF}
\citep{cappellari22a} and \texttt{alf} \citep{conroy18a} extract stellar
kinematics and population parameters from the continuum; spectral energy
distribution fitting codes such as CIGALE \citep{boquien19a}, Bagpipes
\citep{carnall18a}, and Prospector \citep{johnson21a} combine photometry
(and, in some cases, spectroscopy) with physically motivated stellar
population synthesis models to infer star-formation histories, stellar
masses, and dust properties; and tools such as \texttt{pyspeckit}
\citep{ginsburg22a} and \texttt{pyqsofit} \citep{guo18a} are tailored to
fitting emission-line spectra of star-forming galaxies and quasars,
respectively. Each of these codes makes different trade-offs among
flexibility, physical realism, and computational speed, and none was
designed to be run, essentially unsupervised, on tens of millions of
heterogeneous spectra spanning the full diversity of the extragalactic sky.

\fastspecfit\footnote{\url{https://fastspecfit.readthedocs.io}} was
developed to meet this challenge. It is a fully forward-modeling code:
stellar continuum and emission-line templates are convolved with the
wavelength-dependent line-spread function encoded in the DESI resolution
matrix and compared directly to the observed spectra \citep{bolton10a},
fully exploiting the native spectral resolution without degrading the
data to a common wavelength grid. A key algorithmic choice is that
\fastspecfit{} models the stellar continuum and emission lines as
separate components, fitting each in sequence rather than
simultaneously---motivated by the fact that the correlation between the
best-fit continuum model and the emission-line fluxes can be quite
scattered---while ensuring that each component is constrained by the
spectral regions most sensitive to it (\S\ref{sec:modeling}). The
emission-line model additionally supports independent narrow and broad
kinematic components, enabling \fastspecfit{} to characterize the
broad Balmer-line emission that distinguishes type~1 AGN and to measure
the broad wings that can arise in the Balmer lines of star-forming
galaxies and other objects. More broadly, \fastspecfit{} imposes only
the physical constraints the data strictly require---non-negative stellar
population coefficients, an energy-conserving dust model, and kinematic
bounds wide enough to accommodate the full spectral diversity of DESI
targets---and otherwise allows the data to determine the best-fitting
model. Unlike codes designed for a specific target class or spectral
type, it applies the same fitting machinery uniformly to every spectrum,
without prior assumptions about whether the object is a quiescent
elliptical, a star-forming disk galaxy, or an AGN host. This
assumption-minimal design philosophy minimizes classification-driven
selection effects and maximizes the discovery potential of a dataset as
large and heterogeneous as DESI: unusual or physically ambiguous objects
emerge naturally from the fit, rather than requiring special-case
handling or targeted reanalysis. \fastspecfit{} is also engineered for
speed: the code fits a typical DESI spectrum in a few seconds on a
single CPU core, making it practical to process the full DR2
dataset---and to refit it as the data-reduction pipeline and stellar
population models evolve---on modest computing resources.

Although this paper presents the first comprehensive description of
\fastspecfit{}'s algorithms, measurements from the earlier EDR and DR1
value-added catalogs have already enabled a remarkably broad range of
extragalactic science. Emission-line fluxes, equivalent widths, and stellar
masses from \fastspecfit{} have been used to identify and characterize
changing-look AGN \citep{guo25a} and to triple the census of dwarf AGN
candidates in the local universe \citep{pucha25a}; to measure direct-method
($T_e$-based) chemical abundances for nearly 50,000 star-forming galaxies in
DESI DR2 \citep{scholte26a} and to characterize the atomic gas sequence and
mass-metallicity relation across five decades in stellar mass \citep{scholte24a};
to assemble catalogs of post-starburst galaxies \citep{soto25a} and cosmic
voids \citep{rincon25a}; and to identify thousands of strong gravitational
lens candidates via spectroscopic selection \citep{karp26a}. The \fastspecfit{}
measurements have also proved essential to measurements of the galaxy luminosity
function \citep{moore26a}, probabilistic stellar mass functions \citep{hahn24a},
and the modeling of intrinsic alignments and photometric-redshift calibration
for the next generation of weak-lensing surveys. The DR2 value-added catalog
presented here extends this foundation to 37 million extragalactic targets.

In this paper we describe the algorithms underlying \fastspecfit{} in
detail (\S\ref{sec:modeling}), including the spectrophotometric and
physical quantities the code derives (\S\ref{ssec:quantities}), and
illustrate the complete fitting sequence applied end-to-end to a single
representative BGS target (\S\ref{sec:casestudy}).
We then present the \fastspecfit{} value-added catalog for DESI Data Release 2,
comprising 37 million extragalactic targets, along with updated catalogs for
the Early Data Release and Data Release 1 (\S\ref{sec:vac}); this section also
documents the code's computational performance (\S\ref{ssec:performance}) and
validates the measurements against independent codes (\S\ref{ssec:validation}). We conclude with a summary and
an outlook toward future improvements (\S\ref{sec:summary}).

\subsection{DESI Spectroscopy}
\label{ssec:spectra}

DESI \citep{abareshi22a} is a highly multiplexed fiber spectrograph
mounted on the Mayall 4-m telescope at Kitt Peak National Observatory.
Its 5000 robotically actuated fibers subtend a 3.2-deg-diameter field
of view and feed ten three-arm spectrographs covering 3600--9824\,\AA\
in the observed frame \citep{guy23a}. The three cameras sense blue (B:
3600--5930\,\AA), red (R: 5600--7720\,\AA), and near-infrared (Z:
7470--9824\,\AA) light, with a spectral resolution that rises from
$R \approx 2000$ in the blue to $R \approx 5500$ in the near-infrared.

The spectroscopic pipeline \citep{guy23a} delivers flux-calibrated,
sky-subtracted 1D spectra for all targets on a common linear wavelength
grid with 0.8\,\AA\,pixel$^{-1}$ steps. The optimal spectral
extraction \citep[following][]{bolton10a} encodes the wavelength-dependent
line-spread function (LSF) for each fiber in a sparse resolution matrix,
which \fastspecfit{} uses to convolve model spectra before comparing
them to the data (\S\ref{ssec:emlines}).

Wavelength calibration proceeds in three steps: an initial solution
from arc-lamp spectra, a per-exposure PSF and trace refinement, and a
final adjustment via cross-correlation with sky emission lines and an
external high-resolution sky atlas. The residual wavelength
calibration uncertainty after this correction is typically
$\epsilon_\lambda \approx 0.025$\,\AA\ per sky line---roughly 3\%
of a CCD pixel, or $\sim$1\,\kms\ at 7000\,\AA\ \citep{guy23a}.

Flux calibration uses approximately ten F-type standard stars per
spectrograph petal, selected from the Legacy Survey imaging catalogs
on the basis of their $g-r$ and $r-z$ colors \citep{dey19a}. The
pipeline fits stellar spectral models to the standard-star spectra and
derives a wavelength-dependent throughput correction per camera.
Comparison of the calibrated spectra against hydrogen-atmosphere white
dwarf models shows that the residuals are constrained to within
$\pm2\%$ over most of the 3600--9800\,\AA\ wavelength range
\citep{guy23a}.

\subsection{Broadband Photometry}
\label{ssec:photometry}

\fastspecfit{} uses broadband photometry from the DESI Legacy Imaging
Surveys \citep[hereafter Legacy Survey;][]{dey19a}, which cover the
full DESI footprint using two complementary imaging systems: DECam on
the Blanco 4-m telescope at CTIO in the south, and the Mosaic-3
($z$-band) and 90Prime ($g$- and $r$-band) cameras on the Mayall and
Bok telescopes in the north. Both systems deliver photometry in $g$,
$r$, and $z$ bands. We track the imaging system for each target via
the \texttt{PHOTSYS} flag (\texttt{N} or \texttt{S}) to assign the
appropriate filter curves; \fastspecfit{} uses DR9 or DR10 photometry
depending on availability.

The Legacy Survey catalogs also include forced photometry in all four
WISE bands---$W1$ (3.4\,\micron), $W2$ (4.6\,\micron), $W3$
(12\,\micron), and $W4$ (22\,\micron)---derived from unWISE coadds of
the full WISE and NEOWISE mission imaging \citep{wright10a,
meisner21a}. \fastspecfit{} incorporates all four WISE bands in the
spectrophotometric fit; fluxes in bands where the source is
undetected enter the fit as $1\sigma$ upper limits.

The Legacy Survey catalogs provide two distinct photometric
measurements for each source: \emph{total} photometry, which
integrates the full surface brightness profile, and \emph{fiber}
photometry, which estimates the flux a 1.5\arcsec-diameter fiber
would collect at 1\arcsec\ FWHM seeing. \fastspecfit{} fits the
total photometry jointly with the DESI fiber spectrum, but must
reconcile the fact that the fiber samples only the central region of
an extended source. We apply an aperture correction to place the
fiber spectrum on the same flux scale as the total broadband
photometry before the joint fit; we describe this step in
\S\ref{ssec:continuum}.

\subsection{DESI Target Classes and Redshifts}
\label{ssec:targets}

DESI observations are organized by \textit{survey} and
\textit{program} \citep{schlafly23a}. The primary five-year program is
the \textit{main survey}, which began on 2021 May 14; it was preceded
by three survey-validation (SV) phases---Target Selection Validation
(SV1), Operations Development (SV2), and the One-Percent Survey (SV3),
the last of which covered roughly 1\% of the final DESI footprint to
high spectroscopic completeness---documented in detail in the DESI
Early Data Release
\citep[EDR;][]{desi-collaboration24d,desi-collaboration24e}. A
\textit{special} survey accommodates tertiary targets observed on
dedicated tiles outside the standard survey strategy. Within each
survey, observations are assigned to one of three programs on the
basis of sky conditions: the \textit{bright} program (observations
taken in the presence of moonlight or reduced transparency), the
\textit{dark} program (nominal dark-sky conditions), and the
\textit{backup} program (twilight and poor-weather time, devoted
exclusively to stellar targets).

Primary extragalactic targets are photometrically selected from the Legacy
Surveys \citep{dey19a} and organized into four classes spanning complementary
redshift ranges \citep{myers23a}: the Bright Galaxy Survey
\citep[BGS, $0 < z \lesssim 0.6$;][]{hahn23a}, which targets galaxies to
$r \lesssim 20.2$~mag, is observed under bright conditions; and the Luminous
Red Galaxy \citep[LRG, $0.4 < z \lesssim 1.1$;][]{zhou23a}, Emission-Line
Galaxy \citep[ELG, $0.6 < z \lesssim 1.6$;][]{raichoor23a}, and Quasar
\citep[QSO, $0.9 < z \lesssim 4$;][]{chaussidon23a} samples are all observed
under dark conditions. Secondary and tertiary targets supplement these primary
classes for bespoke science programs and can be assigned to any program.

Spectroscopic redshifts and spectral classifications are determined by
Redrock (S.~Bailey et~al., in preparation; \citealt{anand24a}), a
principal component analysis (PCA) template-fitting code that
independently fits templates for stars (\texttt{SPECTYPE=STAR}),
galaxies (\texttt{SPECTYPE=GALAXY}), and quasars
(\texttt{SPECTYPE=QSO}) over a type-dependent grid of trial redshifts
\citep{guy23a}. The two primary redshift-quality diagnostics reported
by Redrock are \texttt{ZWARN}, a per-object warning bitmask where
\texttt{ZWARN=0} signals no known hardware, data-quality, or
redshift-fitting problems, and \texttt{DELTACHI2}, the difference in
$\chi^2$ between the best and second-best fits across all spectral
classes---a value of \texttt{DELTACHI2}~$\geq 25$ corresponds roughly
to $5\sigma$ confidence \citep{desi-collaboration24d}. Three
\textit{after-burner} processing steps follow Redrock: an
emission-line fitter (\texttt{emlinefit}; \citealt{raichoor23a}) used
to validate ELG redshifts; a Mg\,\textsc{ii}~$\lambda\lambda$2796,2803
doublet fitter \citep{chaussidon23a}; and the \texttt{QuasarNET}
neural-network QSO classifier \citep{busca18a}. The latter two are
essential for correctly classifying and measuring accurate redshifts
for QSOs; \fastspecfit{} uses their outputs to correct Redrock
redshifts for a subset of QSO targets (\S\ref{ssec:zqso}).

\section{Spectrophotometric Modeling}
\label{sec:modeling}
%% ============================================================

We begin by describing the two primary model inputs on which the fitting
pipeline depends: the stellar population synthesis template library
(\S\ref{ssec:templates}) and the emission-line list (\S\ref{ssec:emlinelist}).
We then describe the three sequential algorithmic stages of the \fastspec{}
fit in turn: fast fitting in patches (\S\ref{ssec:patches}), stellar continuum
modeling (\S\ref{ssec:continuum}), and emission-line modeling
(\S\ref{ssec:emlines}). A concrete, end-to-end illustration of the complete
sequence applied to a representative BGS target is given in \S\ref{sec:casestudy}.

\subsection{SPS Template Library}
\label{ssec:templates}

\begin{figure*}
\includegraphics[width=6.5in]{figures/sps-models.png}
\caption{The five SPS templates that span the

  the panel show the DECam $grz$ and WISE $W1W2$ broadband filter
  throughputs that \fastspecfit\ includes in the simultaneous
  spectrophotometric fit. \label{fig:sps}}
\end{figure*}

We represent the stellar continuum as a non-negative linear superposition of
simple stellar population (SPS) templates generated with
FSPS\footnote{\url{https://github.com/cconroy20/fsps}}
\citep[Flexible Stellar Population Synthesis;][]{conroy09a,conroy10b}
via the \texttt{python-fsps}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/dfm/python-fsps}}
interface, adopting the C3K empirical stellar spectral library (spanning
2750--9100~\AA\ at $R \equiv \lambda/\mathrm{FWHM} \approx 3000$, or
${\approx}42$~\kms), MIST stellar isochrones \citep{choi16a,dotter16a},
and a Chabrier \citep{chabrier03a} initial mass function (IMF).

We model the star-formation history (SFH) nonparametrically as a
piecewise-constant function over five lookback-time bins, following the
continuity-SFH prescription of \citet{leja17a}: the two youngest bins span
0--30~Myr and 30--100~Myr (with fixed widths of $\Delta\log_{10}(t/\mathrm{yr})
= 7.48$ and $8.0$, respectively), while the three older bins are
logarithmically spaced from 0.1~Gyr to $0.85\,t_\mathrm{univ}$,
corresponding approximately to 0.1--1.1~Gyr, 1.1--11.6~Gyr, and
11.6--13.7~Gyr at $z = 0$. We compute templates at solar metallicity
($\log_{10}Z/Z_\odot = 0$) in two parallel variants---one including and one
excluding nebular emission (continuum and lines) from the FSPS built-in
photoionization grid---so that we can control the nebular contribution
independently during the fitting. Although templates older than the age of
the universe at the target's redshift are, in principle, unphysical, we
include all five age bins by default because the coarse age sampling makes
the exclusion boundary poorly defined \citep{leja19a}.

We normalize each template to the flux density produced at 10~pc by one solar
mass of stars formed at the tabulated lookback time,
\begin{equation}
  F_\lambda\,\bigl[\mathrm{erg\,s^{-1}\,cm^{-2}\,\text{\AA}^{-1}\,M_\odot^{-1}}\bigr]
  \;=\; \frac{L_\odot}{4\pi(10\,\mathrm{pc})^2}\,\frac{L_\lambda}{L_\odot},
  \label{eq:fnorm}
\end{equation}
so that the fitted non-negative coefficients $\mathbf{c} \geq 0$ carry units
of $M_\odot$, up to an overall mass normalization $\mathcal{M}_0 =
10^{10}\,M_\odot$ that sets the internal dynamic range.

We store templates on a hybrid wavelength grid in which pixels span
$\Delta v = 25$~\kms\ in velocity (constant $\Delta\ln\lambda$) over
2750--9100~\AA; outside this range we retain the native, nonuniform FSPS
wavelength grid. The constant-velocity core enables stellar velocity-dispersion
broadening via a fixed-width Gaussian kernel in $\ln\lambda$ space, which we
apply efficiently via FFT, as we describe in \S\ref{sssec:vdisp}.

We additionally include three auxiliary emission components in the template
library: infrared dust emission following the \citet{draine07a} interstellar
dust model, which we normalize to unit bolometric luminosity and scale during
fitting by the absorbed stellar luminosity to enforce energy balance
(\S\ref{sssec:dust}); a \citet{nenkova08a} clumpy AGN torus template,
parameterized by optical depth, which we apply optionally when fitting AGN
host galaxies; and the \citet{vestergaard01a} empirical UV/optical iron
emission template for broad-line AGN. We incorporate all three within the same
non-negative coefficient framework as the SPS templates. Table~\ref{table:templates}
summarizes the full template grid.

\subsection{Emission-Line List}
\label{ssec:emlinelist}

We include 40 emission lines in the \fastspecfit{} line list, spanning
rest-frame Ly$\alpha$~$\lambda$1216 to [\ion{S}{3}]~$\lambda$9532, and
organize them into three fitting categories (Table~\ref{table:emlines}).
Twelve UV and AGN broad lines---from Ly$\alpha$ through
\ion{He}{2}~$\lambda$4686---carry broad, independently fitted kinematics
appropriate for quasar and AGN broad-line emission. Eight Balmer and helium
lines (H6 through H$\alpha$, together with \ion{He}{1}~$\lambda\lambda$4471,
5876) receive narrow kinematic components, and the six Balmer lines (H6
through H$\alpha$) additionally receive independent broad components,
enabling \fastspecfit{} to separate star-forming and AGN contributions to the
Balmer emission. The remaining 20 lines are forbidden transitions, each
fitted with a single narrow Gaussian. We describe the kinematic groupings and
amplitude constraints that link these lines during fitting in
\S\ref{sssec:kinematic}.

\subsection{Fast Fitting in Patches}
\label{ssec:patches}

Before fitting the stellar continuum, we run a lightweight pre-fitting
pass---the \emph{line masker}---that measures empirical line widths and
velocity shifts for each kinematic population and constructs per-line
pixel masks that the continuum fitter uses to exclude
emission-line-contaminated regions.

\begin{figure*}
\includegraphics[width=6.5in]{example-emlines}
\caption{Write me.\label{fig:linefit}}
\end{figure*}

We organize the 40 emission lines (\S\ref{ssec:emlinelist}) into 23
spectrally adjacent \emph{patches}, pre-assigned in the configuration
file \texttt{data/emlines.ecsv}. Each patch groups lines whose
wavelength windows overlap or nearly touch; for example, one patch
contains \hb\ together with [O\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda\lambda$4959,5007,
and another groups \ha\ with [N\,\textsc{ii}]~$\lambda\lambda$6548,6584.
Only patches with at least one line in the observed spectral range are
activated for a given spectrum. Each line carries an \texttt{isstrong}
flag identifying the subset with typical fluxes reliable enough to
measure kinematics; the patch-fitting steps use only strong lines, while
the full list enters the final mask.

The algorithm proceeds in three steps. First, we fit all strong lines
with the narrow-only kinematic model. For each line, we define a pixel
window of $\pm2.5\,\sigma_\mathrm{line}$ centered on the expected
observed-frame wavelength---with a minimum floor of 50~\kms---and
identify local continuum pixels from a surrounding window that we
iteratively expand until at least 11 unmasked pixels are available.
Within each patch we jointly fit line amplitudes and a linear local
continuum; we run two iterations, updating the line-pixel windows with
the best-fit velocity shifts and widths before the second pass. Second,
if any broad Balmer line falls within the observed spectral range, we
repeat the procedure with the narrow+broad model, initializing the broad
Balmer width at 1000~\kms; if any broad Balmer line reaches $S/N > 1.5$
we adopt the broad-model kinematics for masking, and otherwise retain
the narrow-only widths. Third, we rebuild the pixel mask over the full
line list using the converged kinematic parameters, widening the
exclusion window to $\pm5\,\sigma$ to conservatively exclude all
line-contaminated pixels from the continuum fit; weak non-strong lines
with $S/N < 3.5$ are omitted from the final mask to avoid
over-masking. We additionally mask pixels flagged as sky-line residuals
and pixels with zero inverse variance.

The line masker is intentionally fast and approximate: it fits a simple
linear local continuum per patch rather than the full SPS template, uses
only strong lines, and runs for just two iterations with loose
convergence tolerances. Its sole purpose is to supply accurate-enough
pixel masks and kinematic initial guesses for the main continuum and
emission-line fits that follow.

\subsection{Stellar Continuum Model}
\label{ssec:continuum}

We construct the observed-frame stellar continuum model by sequentially applying four
transformations to the SPS templates (\S\ref{ssec:templates}): dust attenuation and
infrared re-emission (\S\ref{sssec:dust}), cosmological redshifting with IGM absorption
(\S\ref{sssec:zfactors}), stellar velocity-dispersion broadening (\S\ref{sssec:vdisp}),
and joint spectrophotometric fitting (\S\ref{sssec:fitting}). For each object we execute
three fitting stages in sequence: we first determine $\vdisp$ from stellar absorption
features; we then apply a per-band aperture correction to bring the fiber spectrum into
agreement with broadband photometry; and finally we perform a joint spectrophotometric
fit for the dust attenuation $\tauv$ and template coefficients $\mathbf{c}$
simultaneously. A nonparametric smooth continuum correction removes low-level residuals
after the primary fit, before the spectrum is passed to the emission-line fitter
(\S\ref{sssec:smooth}).

\subsubsection{Dust Attenuation and Emission}
\label{sssec:dust}

We model the attenuation of the stellar continuum as a uniform foreground screen,
\begin{equation}
  f_\lambda^\mathrm{att}(\lambda)
  \;=\; f_\lambda(\lambda)\,\exp\!\bigl(-\tauv\,k(\lambda)\bigr),
  \label{eq:atten}
\end{equation}
\noindent where
\begin{equation}
  k(\lambda) \;=\; \left(\frac{\lambda}{5500\,\text{\AA}}\right)^{-0.7},
  \label{eq:kl}
\end{equation}

\noindent $f_\lambda$ is the unattenuated template flux, and $\tauv
\in [0,2]$ is the single free parameter, the $V$-band optical
depth. The power-law index $-0.7$ is the \citet{charlot00a}
birth-cloud slope, consistent with the \citet{calzetti00a} attenuation
law shortward of $\sim$6000~\AA.  Absorbed photons are re-emitted in
the infrared following energy balance: we scale the \citet{draine07a}
dust emission template $f_\lambda^\mathrm{dust}$ (normalized to unit
bolometric luminosity) by the absorbed luminosity,
\begin{equation}
  L_\mathrm{abs}
  \;=\; \int_0^\infty f_\lambda(\lambda)
        \bigl[1 - \exp(-\tauv\,k(\lambda))\bigr]\,d\lambda,
\end{equation}
so that the total model conserves bolometric luminosity:
\begin{equation}
  f_\lambda^\mathrm{tot}(\lambda)
  \;=\; f_\lambda^\mathrm{att}(\lambda)
        \;+\; L_\mathrm{abs}\,f_\lambda^\mathrm{dust}(\lambda).
  \label{eq:ftot}
\end{equation}
The dust emission template uses default parameters ($q_\mathrm{PAH}=3.5$,
$U_\mathrm{min}=1.0$, $\gamma=0.01$) and contributes only to the
photometric fit (it falls outside the DESI spectral range for most
targets).

\subsubsection{Redshift, IGM, and Flux Scaling}
\label{sssec:zfactors}

For an object at redshift $z$ with luminosity distance $D_L$, we relate
the rest-frame template to the observed flux density,
\begin{equation}
  f_\lambda^\mathrm{obs}(\lambda)
  \;=\;
  T_\mathrm{IGM}(\lambda,z)\,
  \frac{\mathcal{F}_0\,\mathcal{M}_0}
       {4\pi\,(3.086\times10^{24}\,D_L)^2}
  \,\frac{f_\lambda^\mathrm{tot}(\lambda/(1+z))}{1+z},
  \label{eq:zfactor}
\end{equation}
where $T_\mathrm{IGM}(\lambda,z)$ is the \citet{inoue14a} IGM
transmission accounting for Ly$\alpha$ forest and Lyman-limit
attenuation, $\mathcal{F}_0 = 10^{17}$ converts to our internal flux
units of $10^{-17}$~erg~s$^{-1}$~cm$^{-2}$~\AA$^{-1}$, and
$\mathcal{M}_0 = 10^{10}\,M_\odot$ is the mass normalization. We combine
the scalar prefactor and IGM transmission into a single array
(\texttt{zfactors}) evaluated once per object at initialization and
reused for all template evaluations during fitting.

\subsubsection{Velocity Dispersion}
\label{sssec:vdisp}

We convolve the SPS templates with a Gaussian kernel to model the
line-of-sight stellar velocity dispersion $\vdisp$. On the
constant-velocity wavelength grid ($\Delta v = 25$~\kms\ per pixel), the
kernel has a fixed pixel width $\sigma_\mathrm{pix} = \vdisp/\Delta v$,
enabling efficient FFT-based convolution over the 2750--9100~\AA\
subrange. To avoid recomputing the transform at every trial $\vdisp$, we
pre-compute and cache the Fourier transforms of all templates for $\vdisp
\leq 500$~\kms; convolution then requires only a single inverse FFT per
evaluation. For $\vdisp > 500$~\kms\ we fall back to direct pixel-space
convolution.

We fit $\vdisp$ using only the 3800--6000~\AA\ rest-frame window, which
captures the Ca~H\&K break, $G$-band, Mg~\textsc{b}, and Fe absorption
features sensitive to velocity broadening, and only when the spectrum
satisfies $\lambda_\mathrm{min}^\mathrm{rest} < 3800$~\AA\ and
$\lambda_\mathrm{max}^\mathrm{rest} > 4800$~\AA. The fit uses only
templates without nebular emission and with $\tauv = 0$, which have
negligible effect on the absorption features. We employ a two-stage
strategy: we first perform a $\chi^2$ grid scan over $N = 5$ values of
$\vdisp$ evenly spaced in $[75,\,500]$~\kms, accepting the result only if
$\Delta\chi^2 \geq 25$ and the minimum does not fall at a boundary; if
the scan succeeds, we then refine the estimate with a Trust-Region
Reflective (TRF) optimizer initialized at the grid minimum. When
wavelength coverage or signal-to-noise is insufficient, we fix $\vdisp =
250$~\kms---close to the knee of the velocity-dispersion function at
$z = 0$--$1.5$ \citep[e.g.,][]{bezanson11a}---and use pre-convolved
templates cached as \texttt{flux\_nomvdisp}. This nominal value is an
overestimate for the emission-line galaxies targeted by DESI, but the
effect on the continuum fit is small because those objects are observed
primarily in the rest-frame UV where stellar absorption features are weak.

\subsubsection{Fitting Algorithm}
\label{sssec:fitting}

We fit the stellar continuum by minimizing the weighted sum of squared
spectroscopic and photometric residuals,
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2(\tauv,\vdisp,\mathbf{c})
  \;=\;
  \sum_i \frac{\bigl(f_i^\mathrm{obs} - f_i^\mathrm{model}\bigr)^2}{\sigma_i^2}
  \;+\;
  \sum_k \frac{\bigl(F_k^\mathrm{obs} - F_k^\mathrm{model}\bigr)^2}{\sigma_{f,k}^2},
  \label{eq:chi2cont}
\end{equation}
where the first sum runs over unmasked spectral pixels and the second
over photometric bands with $\sigma_{f,k}^2 > 0$. When $\vdisp$ is
fixed, the model is linear in $\mathbf{c}$ for any fixed $\tauv$; this
separable structure is exploited via variable projection (VARPRO
\citealt{golub73a}): an outer Brent scalar minimization over $\tauv$
(typically 10--15 evaluations) with an inner NNLS solve for
$\mathbf{c}$,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{c}^* \;=\; \arg\min_{\mathbf{c}\,\geq\,0}
  \bigl\|\boldsymbol{\Psi}\,\mathbf{c} - \mathbf{b}\bigr\|^2,
  \label{eq:nnls}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ is the weighted design matrix of
velocity-broadened, attenuated, and synthesized templates, and
$\mathbf{b}$ is the vector of weighted observations (spectra and
photometry stacked). The VARPRO approach converges $\sim$$10\times$
faster than direct optimization over the full parameter vector by
avoiding finite-differencing across all template coefficients. When
$\vdisp$ is a free parameter, the separability is broken and we instead
use \texttt{scipy.optimize.least\_squares} with the TRF method.

Before the full spectrophotometric fit, we estimate a per-band aperture
correction $\eta_f$ from the ratio of observed fiber photometry to
synthesized model photometry from the initial $\vdisp$ fit,
\begin{equation}
  \eta_f \;=\;
  \frac{F_{\lambda,f}^\mathrm{fiber,obs}}{F_{\lambda,f}^\mathrm{model}},
  \label{eq:apercorr}
\end{equation}
and we apply the median $\bar{\eta}$ to scale the fiber spectrum (and its
uncertainties) to total flux before the final joint fit. The full
spectrophotometric fit then uses VARPRO with $\vdisp$ fixed at the value
from \S\ref{sssec:vdisp} and templates that include nebular emission,
fitting the aperture-corrected spectrum and broadband photometry
simultaneously. The aperture correction may not be perfect for QSOs, but
the template flexibility generally recovers the broadband SED shape
\citep{cappellari22a}.

\subsubsection{Smooth Continuum Correction}
\label{sssec:smooth}

The parametric SED model may leave low-level systematic residuals due
to imperfect template matching or flux-calibration uncertainties. We
estimate and remove these with a nonparametric smooth continuum computed
per camera from the spectrum after subtracting the best-fit, line-free
SED model. Working per camera, we slide a 75-pixel window in 125-pixel
steps across the residual spectrum, applying a $2\sigma$ iterative clip
in each window and computing the robust median and inter-quartile sigma;
we discard windows with fewer than 15 unmasked pixels. We then fit a
weighted quadratic spline (\texttt{UnivariateSpline}, $k = 2$) to the
window medians and add the resulting smooth baseline back to the SED
model before emission-line fitting, so that the line fitter operates on
spectra with an accurate continuum level.

\subsection{Emission-Line Model}
\label{ssec:emlines}

\subsubsection{Kinematic Groups}
\label{sssec:kinematic}

We reduce the dimensionality of the emission-line fit by grouping lines
into \emph{kinematic groups}, within which all members share a common
velocity shift $\Delta v$ and line width $s$. Eight UV and broad AGN
permitted lines carry independently free kinematics in both fitting
models: Ly$\alpha$, [N\,\textsc{v}]~$\lambda$1240,
O\,\textsc{i}~$\lambda$1304, Si\,\textsc{iv}~$\lambda$1396,
C\,\textsc{iv}~$\lambda$1549, He\,\textsc{ii}~$\lambda$1640,
Al\,\textsc{iii}~$\lambda$1857, and He\,\textsc{ii}~$\lambda$4686.
Two doublet pairs are treated as global kinematic anchors in both
models: Si\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda$1892 is tied to
C\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda$1908, and Mg\,\textsc{ii}~$\lambda\lambda$2796,2803
shares a single kinematic solution with a free amplitude ratio.

We fit two profile models whose kinematic structure depends on whether a
significant broad Balmer component is present
(\S\ref{sssec:broadlines}). The \emph{narrow-only} model
(\texttt{linemodel\_nobroad}) places all forbidden lines in a single
group anchored to [O\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda$5007, and groups the
narrow Balmer series together with He\,\textsc{i} lines in a second
group anchored to H$\alpha$; all broad Balmer amplitudes are fixed to
zero. The \emph{narrow+broad} model (\texttt{linemodel\_broad})
collapses all narrow lines---Balmer, He\,\textsc{i}, and
forbidden---into a single group anchored to narrow H$\alpha$, retains a
separate kinematic group for [O\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda\lambda$4959,5007,
and adds a third group for broad Balmer emission anchored to broad
H$\alpha$. Narrow-line bounds are $1 < s < 750$~\kms\ and
$|\Delta v| < 500$~\kms; broad-line bounds are
$1 < s < 10{,}000$~\kms\ and $|\Delta v| < 2{,}500$~\kms. We load all
kinematic constraints, amplitude ratios, and optimizer bounds at startup
from the bundled configuration file
\texttt{data/emline-constraints.yaml}.

Several line pairs carry additional constraints on their relative
amplitudes. We fix three doublet ratios to theoretical atomic
emissivities: [N\,\textsc{ii}]~$\lambda$6548/$\lambda$6584 $= 1/3.033$,
[O\,\textsc{iii}]~$\lambda$4959/$\lambda$5007 $= 1/2.964$, and
[O\,\textsc{ii}]~$\lambda$7330/$\lambda$7320 $= 1/1.225$. Three
density-sensitive doublets---[O\,\textsc{ii}]~$\lambda\lambda$3726,3729;
[S\,\textsc{ii}]~$\lambda\lambda$6716,6731; and
Mg\,\textsc{ii}~$\lambda\lambda$2796,2803---carry free amplitude ratios
that encode the electron density.

After the primary constrained fit, \fastspecfit{} runs a second
optimization pass in which individual velocity shifts are freed for each
narrow line, allowing it to absorb small wavelength-calibration
residuals; line widths remain shared within each kinematic group.
Doublet-constrained pairs retain their kinematic ties during the relaxed
pass, since releasing transitions from the same upper level individually
would allow unphysical offsets between their velocity shifts. We adopt
the relaxed solution only when the global $\chi^2$ improvement exceeds
the number of additional free parameters; otherwise we retain the
primary result. Monte Carlo realizations (\S\ref{ssec:uncertainties})
inherit the model structure of the nominal fit.

\subsubsection{Forward Model}
\label{sssec:emline_model}

After subtracting the best-fitting stellar continuum model from the data,
we fit the residual spectrum using a forward model based on log-Gaussian
emission-line profiles coupled to the DESI resolution matrix.

\paragraph{Intrinsic profile.}
The intrinsic spectral profile of a single emission line is a
log-Gaussian in observed wavelength,
\begin{equation}
  f(\lambda;\,A,\lambda^*,\sigma)
  \;=\;
  A\exp\!\left(-\frac{(\ln\lambda - \ln\lambda^*)^{2}}{2\sigma^{2}}\right),
  \label{eq:intrinsic}
\end{equation}
where $A$ is the peak amplitude (in \fluxdensity), $\lambda^*$ is the
observed line center,
\begin{equation}
  \lambda^* \;=\; \lambda_\mathrm{rest}
    \left(1 + z + \frac{\Delta v}{c}\right),
  \label{eq:lstar}
\end{equation}
and $\sigma = s/c$ is the line width in log-$\lambda$ units. The total
line flux is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi \;=\; \sqrt{2\pi}\,A\,\lambda^*\,\sigma
       \;=\; \sqrt{2\pi}\,A\,\lambda^*\,s/c,
  \label{eq:flux_phys}
\end{equation}
which is independent of the instrumental pixel grid and resolution
matrix. \fastspecfit{} reports $\Phi$ as the measured line flux.

\paragraph{Resolution matrix and pre-convolved forward model.}
DESI spectra are produced by an optimal extraction that encodes the
wavelength-dependent line-spread function (LSF) in a sparse, band-format
resolution matrix $\mathbf{R}$ (with $\sim$11 diagonals), normalized to
preserve integrated flux. The standard discrete model
$\mathbf{d} \approx \mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{f}$ (with $\mathbf{f}$ evaluated
at pixel centers) fails for features narrower than approximately one
pixel. A more accurate discretization uses a Gaussian-pre-convolved
spectrum evaluated at pixel centers: defining $\mathbf{W} =
\mathbf{R}\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}$, where $\mathbf{M}_{ij} =
G(\lambda_i - \lambda_j;\,0,\,\sigma_G)$ is a Gaussian design matrix
with width $\sigma_G = 0.5$\,\AA, the predicted extracted spectrum is
$\mathbf{d} \approx \mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{h}$, where
$\mathbf{h}$ is the $\sigma_G$-pre-convolved profile evaluated at pixel
centers \citep[following][]{koposov25a}.

Because the log-Gaussian profile~\eqref{eq:intrinsic} is an exact
Gaussian in log-$\lambda$ space, the convolution with $G_{\sigma_G}$ is
analytic. Defining $x_j = \ln\lambda_j - \ln\lambda^*$ and the
effective width $\sigma_\mathrm{eff} = \sqrt{\sigma^2 + \sigma_{G,\ell}^2}$
(where $\sigma_{G,\ell} = \sigma_G/\lambda^*$), the pre-convolved profile
at pixel center $\lambda_j$ is
\begin{equation}
  F_j \;=\; A\,\frac{\sigma}{\sigma_\mathrm{eff}}
    \exp\!\left(-\frac{x_j^2}{2\sigma_\mathrm{eff}^2}\right).
  \label{eq:model}
\end{equation}
The amplitude factor $\sigma/\sigma_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$ accounts for
broadening by the pre-convolution kernel. The full model spectrum is
$\mathbf{d}^\mathrm{model} = \mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{F}$, summed over all
$N$ emission lines falling within the observed spectral range. Physical
constraints (shared $\Delta v$ and $s$ within kinematic groups; fixed
doublet amplitude ratios) reduce the free-parameter count and improve
reliability for blended or weak lines.

\paragraph{Analytic Jacobian.}
We supply \texttt{scipy.optimize.least\_squares} with closed-form first
derivatives of $F_j$ (equation~\ref{eq:model}) with respect to each
free parameter. Because $F_j$ is linear in $A$, the amplitude gradient
is simply
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial F_j}{\partial A} \;=\; \frac{F_j}{A}.
  \label{eq:dF_dA}
\end{equation}
The velocity-shift gradient follows from the dependence of
$\ln\lambda^* = \ln[\lambda_\mathrm{rest}(1 + z + \Delta v/c)]$ on
$\Delta v$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial F_j}{\partial \Delta v}
  \;=\;
  \frac{F_j\,x_j}{\sigma_\mathrm{eff}^2\,c\,(1+z+\Delta v/c)}.
  \label{eq:dF_dv}
\end{equation}
Using $1/c$ in place of $1/[c\,(1+z+\Delta v/c)]$ would introduce a
fractional error of order $z$ at all redshifts and must be avoided. The
line-width gradient accounts for the dependence of both the amplitude
factor $\sigma/\sigma_\mathrm{eff}$ and the exponent on $\sigma = s/c$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial F_j}{\partial s}
  \;=\;
  \frac{A}{\sigma_\mathrm{eff}\,c}\,
  e^{-x_j^2/(2\sigma_\mathrm{eff}^2)}\,
  \Bigl[1 - \sigma^2\bigl(1 - x_j^2/\sigma_\mathrm{eff}^2
  \bigr)/\sigma_\mathrm{eff}^2\Bigr].
  \label{eq:dF_ds}
\end{equation}
This form evaluates to a finite limit as $\sigma \to 0$, unlike
equivalent expressions containing an explicit $1/\sigma$. Supplying
these analytic derivatives eliminates finite-difference gradient
evaluations and reduces the number of forward-model calls per optimizer
iteration from $\mathcal{O}(N_\mathrm{param})$ to one.

We implement the forward model and Jacobian using Numba JIT compilation
\citep[v\,\todo{X.Y};][]{lam15a}, which compiles the pixel-level
evaluation loops to native machine code at first invocation.

\paragraph{Fitting.}
We minimize
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2 = \sum_{j} I_{j}\bigl(d_j - d_j^\mathrm{model}\bigr)^2,
  \label{eq:chi2line}
\end{equation}
where $d_j$ is the continuum-subtracted observed flux (in \fluxdensity)
and $I_j$ is the corresponding inverse variance (in \ivardensity), using
the trust-region reflective algorithm
\citep[\texttt{scipy.optimize.least\_squares};][]{branch99a}. Parameter
uncertainties are estimated from the diagonal of the covariance matrix
returned by the optimizer \citep{andrae10a}. Line fluxes and their
uncertainties are computed analytically from the fitted
$(A,\,\lambda^*,\,s)$ via equation~\eqref{eq:flux_phys}, without
reference to the pixel sampling or the resolution matrix. We flag a line
as a significant detection when $A\,\sqrt{I_A} > 1.5$, where $I_A$ is
the inverse variance of the observed flux density at the line center;
lines falling below this threshold are reported as $1\sigma$ upper
limits, with continuum flux densities interpolated from the best-fitting
SED model. Equivalent widths use the same SED-interpolated continuum.

\subsubsection{Broad-Line Decomposition}
\label{sssec:broadlines}

\fastspecfit{} fits every spectrum with both the narrow-only and
narrow+broad models simultaneously, then selects the preferred solution
based on four conditions that must all be satisfied. First, the
reduction in $\chi^2$ over the pixels spanning the broad Balmer lines
must exceed the corresponding reduction in degrees of freedom,
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\chi^2_\mathrm{Balmer} \;>\; \Delta N_\mathrm{dof,\,Balmer},
  \label{eq:broadchi2}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\chi^2 = \chi^2_\mathrm{narrow} - \chi^2_\mathrm{broad}$
and $\Delta N_\mathrm{dof}$ counts the additional free parameters
introduced by the broad-Balmer group, evaluated only over pixels within
$\pm N_\sigma$ of the non-helium broad Balmer lines. Second, the fitted
velocity dispersion of the broad \ha\ component must exceed
$\sigma_\mathrm{broad}(\ha) > 250$~\kms, excluding spurious broad fits
driven by noise or continuum residuals. Third, the broad component must
be wider than the narrow component,
$\sigma_\mathrm{broad}(\ha) > \sigma_\mathrm{narrow}(\ha)$, ensuring a
physical hierarchy between the two kinematic components. Fourth, at
least one of the two brightest broad Balmer lines in the spectral range
must satisfy $S/N_\mathrm{broad} > 2.5$, where the noise is estimated
robustly from the interquartile range of the fit residuals in the
Balmer-line pixels after subtracting the full broad model. If any
condition fails, we retain the narrow-only result. The pixel-level
$\chi^2$ comparison is recorded in the output columns
\texttt{DELTA\_LINECHI2} and \texttt{DELTA\_LINENDOF}.

After the broad/narrow model selection, \fastspecfit{} runs the
optional kinematic relaxation pass described in
\S\ref{sssec:kinematic}. For the narrow-only model, individual velocity
shifts are freed within a $\pm100$~\kms\ window around each line's
primary best-fit value, while line widths remain tied; this
configuration accommodates galaxies with forbidden-line outflows, where
the forbidden and Balmer lines often settle at slightly different
systemic velocities. For the narrow+broad model, no kinematic parameters
are relaxed: the three-group structure already absorbs most residual
complexity through the broad Balmer component, and freeing kinematics in
that model risks over-fitting. In both cases we adopt the relaxed
solution only when the $\chi^2$ improvement weighted by the degrees of
freedom exceeds the degrees of freedom added by the relaxation;
otherwise we retain the primary result. We record this comparison in the
output columns \texttt{DELTA\_KINECHI2} and \texttt{DELTA\_KINENDOF}.

\subsection{Spectrophotometric and Physical Properties}
\label{ssec:quantities}

We derive several physical quantities from the best-fitting continuum model and
report them in the output catalog (\S\ref{sec:appendix_datamodel}). We compute
the stellar mass as the sum of the NNLS template coefficients $\mathbf{c}$,
which we weight by the FSPS surviving-mass fractions that account for the mass
stellar evolution returns to the ISM via winds and supernovae. We caution that
non-parametric SFH models can yield stellar masses that are systematically
higher---by $\sim$0.3--0.4~dex on average---than parametric estimates, because
a flexible SFH can accommodate more mass in old, low-luminosity stellar
populations \citep{leja19a,leja20a}. We estimate the instantaneous
star-formation rate (SFR) from the coefficient of the youngest age bin
(0--30~Myr), dividing by the width of that bin to obtain the mean SFR averaged
over the most recent 30~Myr of star-formation history. Although \fastspecfit{}
measures both the H$\alpha$ luminosity and the rest-frame UV continuum---each of
which serves as an independent SFR diagnostic---we do not currently convert
either quantity to a SFR within the code.

We compute K-corrections by comparing synthesized observer-frame and rest-frame
photometry from the best-fit SED model \citep{hogg02a,blanton07a}, and we derive
rest-frame absolute magnitudes in each Legacy Survey band via the standard
distance-modulus relation. We additionally report monochromatic luminosities at
1450, 1500, 1700, 2800, 3000, and 5100~\AA, which we interpolate from the
best-fit model and which are especially useful for UV-selected samples and AGN
host-galaxy studies.

We compute equivalent widths (EWs) for all modeled emission lines as the ratio
of the integrated line flux to the local continuum flux density, which we
interpolate from the best-fit SED model; this choice ensures that EW estimates
remain insensitive to continuum-subtraction noise in the observed spectrum. We
measure the $4000$~\AA\ break strength $D_n(4000)$ \citep{balogh99a} in two
complementary ways: from the best-fit model alone, which is noise-free but
sensitive to template mismatch, and directly from the observed spectrum after
subtracting the best-fit emission-line model, which is noisy but
model-independent. We report both estimates, which together provide a
consistency check on the stellar population fit.

\subsection{Parameter Uncertainties}
\label{ssec:uncertainties}

We estimate parameter uncertainties by repeating the continuum fit on
$N_\mathrm{monte} = 50$ independent Gaussian realizations of the
observed spectra and photometry,
\begin{equation}
  f_i^{(m)} \;\sim\; \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(f_i^\mathrm{obs},\,\sigma_i^2\bigr),
  \qquad m = 1,\ldots,N_\mathrm{monte},
\end{equation}
drawn with a fixed random seed and reused for both the continuum and
emission-line fits. The inverse variance of each fitted quantity
$\theta$ is then
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_\theta^{-2}
  \;=\; \bigl[\mathrm{Var}(\theta^{(1)},\ldots,\theta^{(N_\mathrm{monte})})\bigr]^{-1}.
  \label{eq:mc_unc}
\end{equation}
We report Monte Carlo uncertainties for \tauv, \vdisp\ (when fitted),
$D_n(4000)$, rest-frame luminosities, and observed-frame continuum flux
densities.

\subsection{Additional Fitting Modes}
\label{sec:modes}

\fastspecfit{} operates in two modes. \fastphot{} fits broadband spectral
energy distributions (SEDs) to Legacy Survey DR9/DR10 photometry with no
spectroscopic data. \fastspec{} jointly fits spectra from all three DESI
cameras (B, R, Z) and broadband photometry simultaneously. Both modes
share the same template library, dust model, and physical output
quantities; they differ only in the data that enter the fit and in
whether the stellar velocity dispersion is a free parameter.

\subsection{Sample Selection}
\label{ssec:sample}

We model all extragalactic DESI targets that satisfy three selection criteria: a
spectroscopic redshift $z > 10^{-3}$, a non-sky target class
(\texttt{OBJTYPE}~=~\texttt{TGT}), and a valid spectrum
(\texttt{ZWARN}~\&~\texttt{NODATA}~=~0, where the \texttt{NODATA} bit flags
spectra in which all extracted inverse-variance values are zero). No cuts are
imposed on targeting bits or other redshift-warning bits, ensuring that
\fastspecfit{} results are available for the widest possible range of objects
across every DESI target class.

\subsection{Updated QSO Redshifts}
\label{ssec:zqso}

For a small but astrophysically important fraction of quasar targets, the
\texttt{Redrock} spectral classification pipeline assigns an incorrect redshift.
We correct these using the \texttt{QuasarNet} and MgII afterburner catalogs
distributed alongside each \texttt{Redrock} output file. For primary QSO
targets, we replace the \texttt{Redrock} redshift when two conditions are met:
\texttt{QuasarNet} independently classifies the object as a QSO, and the maximum
line confidence across six diagnostic transitions (Ly$\alpha$, C\,\textsc{iv},
C\,\textsc{iii}], Mg\,\textsc{ii}, \hb, and \ha) exceeds 0.99 for DR2 (0.95 for
DR1 and the EDR). A broader criterion is applied to variable WISE QSO secondary
targets; full details are documented in the online VAC documentation. The
original \texttt{Redrock} redshift and \texttt{ZWARN} bitmask are preserved in
the output columns \texttt{Z\_RR} and \texttt{ZWARN\_RR}. In total, corrected
redshifts are applied to approximately 9,100 targets in the EDR, 74,000 in DR1,
and 169,000 in DR2.

Table~\ref{table:sample} summarizes the number of targets satisfying these
criteria in each DESI data release, organized by survey and observing program.

\subsection{DR2 Catalog and Data Format}
\label{ssec:dr2}

We produced the DR2 value-added catalog (Loa~v1.0) using \fastspecfit{}
version~3.1.4 with the \texttt{ftemplates-chabrier-2.0.0.fits} SPS template

VAC (Fuji~v3.2, \fastspecfit{}~v2.5.0) comprises approximately 1.4~million

campaigns and was publicly released in January~2024. The DR1 VAC

June~2025. The DR2 VAC encompasses the complete five-year main survey and totals
approximately 38~million targets, of which the main survey---split between
roughly 13~million BGS targets observed under bright conditions and 23~million
LRG, ELG, and QSO targets observed under dark conditions---contributes

key differences in template versions and algorithmic choices relative to DR2, in

We run \fastspecfit{} on the HEALPix-coadded DESI spectra and organize the
output catalogs identically to how the underlying spectra, redshift catalogs,
and other data products are organized within DESI data releases. Each VAC
contains two types of output: individual per-HEALPix files and merged catalogs.
We locate the per-HEALPix files, relative to the top-level VAC directory

\medskip
\noindent\texttt{VACDIR/healpix/SURVEY/PROGRAM/HPIXGROUP/HEALPIX/}\\
\texttt{\quad \{fastspec,fastphot\}-SURVEY-PROGRAM-HEALPIX.fits\{.gz\}}
\medskip

\noindent where \texttt{SURVEY}, \texttt{PROGRAM}, \texttt{HPIXGROUP}, and
\texttt{HEALPIX} follow the same conventions as the DESI data products. We
gzip-compress the \texttt{fastspec} HEALPix catalogs because they include a
\texttt{MODELS} extension containing the best-fitting model spectra (see
\S\ref{sec:appendix_datamodel}); we do not compress the \texttt{fastphot}
catalogs because they carry no \texttt{MODELS} extension.

Most users will find it more convenient to work with the merged catalogs, which
we assemble by combining all per-HEALPix files for a given \texttt{SURVEY} and
\texttt{PROGRAM} into a single file:

\medskip
\noindent\texttt{VACDIR/catalogs/\{fastspec,fastphot\}-SURVEY-PROGRAM.fits}
\medskip

\noindent We omit the \texttt{MODELS} extension from the merged
\texttt{fastspec} catalogs to keep individual file sizes manageable. For large
survey-program combinations---specifically \texttt{main-bright} and
\texttt{main-dark}---we further subdivide the merged catalogs by
\texttt{nside}~=~1 HEALPixel into 12 files following the naming pattern
\texttt{\{fastspec,fastphot\}-SURVEY-PROGRAM-nside1-hpNN.fits}, where
\texttt{NN} runs from \texttt{00} to \texttt{11}. We describe the complete
output data model for each FITS extension in \S\ref{sec:appendix_datamodel},
and we make all catalogs publicly accessible via
\url{https://data.desi.lbl.gov}.

clipping error in the internal flux uncertainty routine. We fix both issues in
\fastspecfit{}~v3.2.0, and we will release updated catalogs in due course.

The \fastspecfit{} DR2 catalog provides simultaneous measurements of stellar
continuum, emission-line, and broadband SED properties for approximately
38~million extragalactic targets spanning four decades in stellar mass and
nearly two decades in redshift. In this section we illustrate the scientific
reach of these measurements with five representative figures: the distribution
of stellar mass with redshift across the three primary galaxy target classes
(\S\ref{ssec:mz}); the star-forming main sequence and the EW(\oii)--$D_n(4000)$

\section{DR2 VAC Data Model}
\label{sec:appendix_datamodel}

The \fastspecfit{} output is stored in a compressed FITS file
named \texttt{fastspec-\textit{SURVEY}-\textit{PROGRAM}-\textit{HEALPIX}.fits.gz},
where \textit{SURVEY} (e.g., \texttt{main}), \textit{PROGRAM}
(e.g., \texttt{bright} or \texttt{dark}), and \textit{HEALPIX} identify
the observing program and sky region. Each file contains five
extensions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{HDU0} (\texttt{PRIMARY}): empty image with processing
    metadata keywords, including the software version, Monte Carlo
    random seed, and boolean fitting flags.
  \item \texttt{HDU1} (\texttt{METADATA}): target metadata mirroring
    the DESI redshift catalog, including sky coordinates, the input
    redshift and zwarning bitmask, targeting bits, and extinction-corrected
    Legacy Survey photometry.
  \item \texttt{HDU2} (\texttt{SPECPHOT}): derived spectrophotometric
    quantities, including SFH template coefficients, stellar-population
    parameters ($\tau_V$, $\vdisp$, age, metallicity, $\logmstar$, SFR),
    $D_n(4000)$, synthesized photometry, K-corrections, absolute
    magnitudes, monochromatic luminosities, and continuum flux densities
    at key emission-line centers.
  \item \texttt{HDU3} (\texttt{FASTSPEC}): emission-line fitting results,
    including per-camera median S/N and smooth continuum corrections,
    aperture correction factors, doublet line ratios, and per-line
    measurements (amplitude, flux, equivalent width, velocity shift,
    line width, and upper limits) for all modeled emission lines.
  \item \texttt{HDU4} (\texttt{MODELS}): 3D image array
    [$N_\lambda \times 3 \times N_\mathrm{obj}$] with the best-fitting
    model spectra on a common linear wavelength grid (3600--9824\,\AA,
    $0.8$\,\AA\,pixel$^{-1}$, vacuum wavelengths). The three planes
    store the full spectrophotometric model, the smooth continuum
    correction alone, and the emission-line model alone, respectively.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{table:datamodel} describes the key output columns in the
\texttt{SPECPHOT} and \texttt{FASTSPEC} extensions. All integrated
line fluxes are in units of \flux, flux densities in \fluxdensity, and
uncertainties are stored as inverse variances (a value of zero indicates
the parameter was not measured or the uncertainty is undefined).
A machine-readable description of the complete data model is maintained
in the \fastspecfit{} online
documentation.\footnote{\url{https://fastspecfit.readthedocs.io/en/latest/fastspec.html}}

\section{Pipeline Diagnostics}
\label{sec:appendix_diagnostics}

One of the dividends of fitting every DESI spectrum with a physically
motivated forward model is that the residuals carry information about
the quality of the underlying data reduction. In this appendix we use
\fastspecfit{} measurements to characterize two classes of residual
systematic error in the DR2 pipeline: wavelength-solution errors
revealed by per-camera emission-line velocity shifts
(\S\ref{ssec:waveresid}), and spectrophotometric calibration residuals
revealed by the difference between synthesized and observed broadband
photometry as a function of magnitude, redshift, and target class
(\S\ref{ssec:fluxresid}).

\subsection{Wavelength Calibration Residuals}
\label{ssec:waveresid}

\fastspecfit{} fits each of the three DESI cameras (B, R, Z) separately
and records the median emission-line velocity shift per camera for every
spectrum in which emission lines are detected. For a perfectly calibrated
wavelength solution, this per-camera shift should be consistent with zero
and with the global redshift measured by Redrock. Systematic offsets or
trends with wavelength, fiber position, or observing conditions indicate
residual errors in the arc-lamp or sky-line wavelength calibration.
